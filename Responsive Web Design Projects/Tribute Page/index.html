Dr. Mae Jemison

The First Black Female Astronaut

A Brief Timeline of a Remarkable Woman

  • 1956 - Born in Decatur, Alabama.
  • 1972 - Graduated high school and began attending Stanford University, on a scholarship, at the young age of 16.
  • 1977 - Graduated from Stanford University with a B.S. in Chemical Engineering. After struggling, her senior year, with the decision to pursue a career in choreography or attend medical school.
  • 1981 - Received her doctorate in medicine from Cornell University.
  • 1983 - Served as a general practioner on Peace Corps missions to Sierra Leone and Liberia for two years.
  • 1985 - She first applied to NASA's Astronaut program, but the Space Shuttle Challenger disaster of 1986 postponed selections.
  • 1987 - Reapplied to the Astronaut program and was selected from roughly 2,000 candidates as one of the fifteen people to compose NASA Astronaut Group 12.
  • 1989 - Selected as a crew member of STS-47 as a Science Mission Specialist, a new role focusing on scientific experiments.
  • 1992 - Flew her only space mission from September 12 to 20; logging 190 hours, 30 minutes, and 23 seconds in space, and orbiting the earth 127 times. A fan of Star Trek, she would open her shift communications with "Hailing frequencies open," a quote from the sci-fi show.
  • 1993 - Resigned from NASA to start her own company, to the regret of her former training manager.
  • 1994 to present - After her brief tenure as an Astronaut, Dr. Jemison went on to serve the medical and science industry in many areas. She founded The Jemison Group Inc. and the Dorothy Jemison Foundation, organizations which focus on science and technology. She currently leads the 100-Year Starship Program, a project which researches interstellar travel.

"Never be limited by other people's limited imaginations...If you adopt their attitudes, then the possibility won't exist because you'll have already shut it out ... You can hear other people's wisdom, but you've got to re-evaluate the world for yourself." ~ Mae Jemison, at the Annual Biomedical Research Conference for Minority Students, November 2009
